    Origin

    Doge to Doggo, the doge that started it all and the video that went viral.

    In 2013 a picture posted by a Japanese Kindergarten teacher of her Shiba Inu named Kabosu turned into an internet sensation. The "mona-lisa"-esque, ambiguious facial expression of the dog seems to be the initial attraction. It sparked a global meme using comic-sans font and grammatically incorrect English.

    Here we will learn how to embed an image in HTML.

    <img src="URL" alt="describe image">

    Note that images do not need closing tags. It is important to always have an alt with a short descriptive phrase to explain the image. This will help with accessibility for visually impaired users and will be a stand-in if the image cannot load.

    Some might say that what truly made the doge phenomena take off (pun intended) was when "Doge Adventure" went viral on YouTube. With 2.2 million views, "Doge Adventure" is a cartoon of Kobasu on an adventure through the atmosphere. Video clip below.

    Here we will take a look at inserting this video into our index.html using iframe.

    <iframe src="URL" id="description" height="px value" width="px value" allowfullscreen> </iframe>

    Adding a height and width is not necessary, but may be useful when inserting a video into your webpage. The 'allowfullscreen' is also a useful tool that should be added before your closing tag.

    After the virility of "Doge Adventure" there was a lag before the dawn of the doggo. Doggo was most likely a term popularized by the Instagram account @WeRateDogs, but originally hailes from the land down under. In Australia they like to short form or make up cute nicknames for pretty well everything, and doggo seems to be part of the vernacular. One internet phrase lead to another and now we have our own Doggo Speak.

    Types of Doggo

    Since the dawn of doggo, there have been many new words created to describe different types of doggos. There are many, many different words to describe different sorts and breeds of our four legged friends, but for the purposes of this page we will stick to the most commonly used terms. Here is a list of doggo terms that refer to doggos by their general aesthetic and tempermant.

    • Doggo - your average everyday dog
    • Pupper - doggo youth, the puppies
    • Borker - small but loud and proud
    • Floof - much fur everywhere
    • Woofer - lorge and loud

    We will use this, types of doggo, list to illustrate how to make unordered lists in HTML.

    <ul> (unoredered list)
    <li> "list item 1" </li>
    <li> "list item 2" </li>
    etc.
    </ul>

    It is important to note that each individual list item requires an opening and closing tag to ensure it has its own bullet point and line as well as the entire unordered list needing opening and closing tags to make it a whole.

    Next we will look at a list of doggo terms that typically refer to specific breeds of doggos.

    1. Goldenboi - Golden Retriever
    2. Longboi - Greyhound (or Russian wolfhounds)
    3. Cloud - Samoyed
    4. Puggerino - Pug

    Here we have an ordered list. This is how to make an ordered list in HTML.

    <ol> (ordered list)
    <li> "list item 1" </li>
    <li> "list item 2" </li>
    etc.
    </ol>

    An ordered list functions exactly the same as an unordered list with the exception that each list item is numbered. In an ordered list you can reverse the numbers to count down instead of up by adding "reversed" to the ol tag. You can also add a value = "#" to the first list item to start the count from a number other than one.

    <ol> reversed
    <li> value=10 "list item 10" </li>
    <li> "list item 9" </li>
    etc.
    </ol>
    Doggo Noises

    Besides coming up with words to describe different types of doggos, the internet has also come up with some words that help us understand what kind of noise the doggo is making. The most common of these are:

    • Woof - common with lorgebois has a deep timbre
    • Bork - comes in all sizes, but often the smol doggos are known to bork
    • Awoo - almost exclusively with huskos but some other howlers too
    • Boof - the snorting, exhaling sound, often made when tracking small animals like squirrels
